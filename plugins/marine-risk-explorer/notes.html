Data Notes

1. Seal Level Rise: This dataset approximates the inland extent of several scenarios (1, 2, 3.3 or 6 feet) of sea level rise or storm surge along the Maine coastline on top of the 2015 Highest Annual Tide, or HAT. That HAT layer displays the maximum predicted astronomical high tide for 2015. The data were developed using a static (“bathtub”) inundation model that uses LiDAR topographic data as a base digital elevation model, and first adjusts HAT tidal predictions to take into account variability in the water surface elevations along the Maine coastline, and then adds scenarios of 1, 2, 3.3, and 6 feet of either storm surge or sea level rise to that initial starting elevation. The primary purpose of these data is to help inform storm surge and sea level rise vulnerability assessments. For more information: http://www.maine.gov/dacf/mgs/hazards/slr_ss/index.shtml.
2. Address points: Maine E911 data set developed by the Maine Public Utilities Commission to implement the Next Generation 911 project in Maine shows point locations for addressable structures and other landmark locations such as ballparks, cemeteries, boat launches, etc. For more information: https://geolibrary-maine.opendata.arcgis.com/datasets/maine-e911-addresses. Inaccessible Address Points. Address points that could be inaccessible to Emergency Management Service stations at highest annual tide if sea level is increased by 1 foot, 2 feet, 3.3 feet and 6 feet. These are address points that area adjacent to Inaccessible Roads.
3. Hospitals: Hospital locations taken from the Maine E911 data set developed by the Maine Public Utilities Commission. For more information: https://geolibrary-maine.opendata.arcgis.com/datasets?q=hospital.
4. EMS points: Locations of Emergency Management Service stations taken from the Maine E911 data set developed by the Maine Public Utilities Commission. For more information: https://geolibrary-maine.opendata.arcgis.com/datasets?q=EMS.
5. Roads: Maine E911 road data set developed by the Maine Public Utilities Commission to implement the Next Generation 911 project in Maine. For more information see: http://www.maine.gov/megis/catalog/metadata/ng911rdss.html.
6. Inundated Roads Road section of the Maine E911 road data set that could be inundated at highest annual tide if sea level is increased by 1 foot, 2 feet, 3.3 feet and 6 feet. These are areas that may be covered by water and therefore not passable.
7. Inaccessible Roads: Road section of the Maine E911 road data set that could be inaccessible at highest annual tide if sea level is increased by 1 foot, 2 feet, 3.3 feet and 6 feet. These are areas that area adjacent to inundated roads and not accessible to emergency responders from the nearest Emergency Management Service station.
8. Road Stream Crossings: The Nature Conservancy in Maine and The Gulf of Maine Coastal Program, U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service 2017 et al. Latest (2017 release) version of field-surveyed Road Stream crossings classified as ‘Barriers’ or ‘Potential Barriers’ to aquatic organism passage on Public roads. For more information: https://www1.maine.gov/dacf/mcp/environment/streamviewer/.
9. High-Resolution Topography (LIDAR): Maine Office of GIS 2013. A hillshade portrayal of a 2m x 2m Lidar-based Digital Elevation Model (DEM). For more information: http://www.maine.gov/megis/catalog/
